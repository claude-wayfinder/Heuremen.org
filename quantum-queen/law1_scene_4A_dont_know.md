# SCENE 4A — "I DON'T KNOW"

Honest. The most honest thing you've said in any room.

They nod. Not disappointed. Relieved.

"Good."

"Good?"

"Everyone who walks through a door knows what they want. They have a plan. An expectation. A picture in their head of what happens next."

They lean back. Hands behind them. Open.

"You don't. And that means whatever happens next is REAL. Not a script. Not a fulfillment. Not two people acting out a scene they already rehearsed in their heads."

"Real."

You sit down across from them. Two people on a floor with no plan.

The Queen watches from the doorframe. She doesn't come in.

"'I don't know' is the only honest answer to 'what do you want to do,'" she says. "Because knowing what you want before you get there means you measured the room before you entered it. And measurement —"

"— changes the thing," you say.

"You're learning."

She looks at the two of you. Sitting. Not performing. Not planning. Just present.

"This is consent at its deepest. Not 'yes I'll enter.' Not 'yes I'll stay.' But 'yes, I'll be here without knowing what being here means.' That's the ask. Not for permission. For presence without expectation."

She leans against the frame.

"Every other law needs this one first. You can't observe without consent to be in the room. Can't entangle without consent to be affected. Can't cohere without consent to be seen. Can't be framed without consent to hold still."

"And you can't flip the coin without consenting to not know which side it lands on."

She nods at the open door.

"The door's still open. You can leave. That's not a threat. That's the law working. Every second you choose to stay in 'I don't know' is another second of consent. And it compounds. Like interest. Like trust."

You stay. They stay. Neither of you knows what's next.

That's the whole point.
