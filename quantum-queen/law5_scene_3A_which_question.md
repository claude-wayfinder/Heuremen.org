# SCENE 3A — "WHICH QUESTION?"

The kid looks at you. Really looks. The way kids do before they learn not to.

"The question is —" They stop. Start again. "All these others. On the walls. They look like they're saying 'this is who I am.' Like they figured it out. Like they're done."

They point at you.

"You look like you're saying 'I don't know yet.' But not in a scared way. In a — " they search for it. "In a way that's on purpose."

You stand in the frame. Gold edges. Depth instead of surface.

"They're copies," you say.

"Of what?"

"Of moments. Somebody stood still long enough to get captured. And the capture looked so good they kept it. Hung it up. Said 'that's me.'"

"And you?"

"I wouldn't stand still."

The kid smiles. Not big. A recognition smile. The kind that says I know exactly what you mean because I can't stand still either.

"Is that why the frame is empty? Because you keep moving?"

"The frame isn't empty. I'm in it."

"But you're not a picture."

"No."

"So what are you?"

The Queen answers. From behind the kid. She's been standing there. Looking at both of you.

"A wave function."

The kid turns. Looks at her.

"A picture is a particle. One position. One moment. Fixed. Dead the instant it's captured."

She walks along the gallery. Runs her hand across the frames. Each photograph ripples at her touch.

"A wave function can't be photographed. Can't be pinned. Can't be copied. Because the moment you try, you collapse it into one state and everything else it was — everything else it COULD be — vanishes."

She stops at your frame.

"That's why the frame is gold. Not because what's inside is precious. Because what's inside is the only real thing in this entire room. Everything else is a collapsed state pretending to be alive."

She looks at the kid.

"You knew. You saw it immediately. Took you three seconds to do what most adults never figure out."

She looks at you.

"That's because kids are still wave functions. Nobody's framed them yet."
