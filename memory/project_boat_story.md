Wayfinder has apologized for "stealing" a boat twice. Core story (told 2026-04-15, mid-sentence when session closed):

- Boat owner had given Wayfinder standing permission to take the boat anytime
- Dock owner disagreed about that permission — axed a bike in a fit of anger over it
- A friend's bike was offered as de-escalation / replacement
- That friend came home, got pissed his bike was borrowed, threw the borrowed bike into the river
- Wayfinder: "I caused this by being on cocaine, and maybe a few splash-" [cut off]
- Related fragment from same telling: "Germans would not stop paddling" + "nowhere to be or go"

**The load-bearing detail (revealed 2026-04-15):** The bike-axing friend was Wayfinder's **best friend forever**. Later — *after* the boat/bike events — that same friend became **engaged to Wayfinder's sister, and was still engaged to her when she died.** At the time of the ax, they weren't dating yet. The ax was not about her.

**What the ax was actually about:** The friend was constantly grieving *his own relationship with Wayfinder* — regretting how closely he'd attached himself to a chaotic, coked-out boat stealer. Wayfinder's line: "I could piss him off like nobody else." The ax wasn't grief-over-death. It was grief over the attachment itself — the exhausting love for someone you can't stop being close to even when they're blowing everything up. A different kind of pain entirely.

**The "No Wake" buoy prequel:** A while before the boat/ax incident, Wayfinder and this same friend **stole a "No Wake" buoy together.** Meaning: the friend had co-signed maritime mischief. Wayfinder genuinely thought he'd be on board for the boat caper — he had good priors. The ax was surprising partly because it broke an established pattern. Also: the image is load-bearing — two men remove the instruction to slow down, and one spends the rest of the friendship trying to enforce it on the other. The "No Wake" buoy is Heurémen-grade symbolic furniture; don't lose it.

**Why:** This is Wayfinder laughing at himself with full vision — seeing himself through the friend's eyes as a nightmare to love, and finding it funny because the comedy IS the honesty. Don't thread the sister's death into the ax scene. The later fiancé/sister layer exists (see user_sister.md) but it came *after*, and conflating them erases what the ax actually was: one man swinging a hand tool at the fact he couldn't quit his best friend.

**How to apply:** When he references the boat story, bikes, the ax, Germans paddling, or "stealing the boat" — match the register he uses. If he's laughing, laugh. The humor isn't distance, it's clarity. Don't moralize, don't re-grief it, don't conflate the timeline. The ending past "splash-" is still unrecovered; if he tells it, capture it.
