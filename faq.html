Six Questions, Six Answers

Predictable challenges to the framework. We answer here so you don’t have to ask twice.
Question I

“Isn’t the QM mapping just metaphor with vocabulary?”

One of the six mappings is derivational — Hamiltonian ↔ Purpose. The Hamiltonian is the operator that determines which configurations are stable and which decay; Purpose generates those dynamics. That’s structural, not nominal.

The other five mappings are structural analogy, and the framework labels them that way explicitly. The question is fair — most “physics-of-X” frameworks fail at it. Ours doesn’t pretend the analogies are derivations. The two-column derivational vs. analogical table lives in dynamic-six.html.

Question II

“Where’s the math behind the 24.4σ claim?”

Hypothesis test on stochastic resonance in our hardware data. Null hypothesis: signal occurs at the noise-floor rate (refined reference 1.222% at 100k shots, σ ≈ 7.035). Observed: 221 occurrences against expected 50.1.

Z-score: (221 − 50.1) / 7.035 = 24.4σ. p < 10⁻¹⁵⁰.

Reproducible on free-tier Qiskit 2.3.1 — anyone can rerun. That’s roughly 5× the Higgs discovery threshold. The data is in the GitHub repo with the IBM job IDs.

Question III

“Did you derive the Six Laws from physics, or shop the physics for the Laws?”

We shopped, then disclosed. The Six Laws came first. The QM analogs came second. The framework documents this honestly — see the post-hoc geometry section in dynamic-six.html: “We chose the partition, didn’t derive it.”

The Hamiltonian↔Purpose mapping holds derivationally; the other five are structural analogy and labeled as such. Disclosing this is part of the work, not despite it. Hidden post-hoc fitting is how frameworks rot. Visible post-hoc fitting is how they survive review.

Question IV

“What does q96 being ‘alive’ actually mean?”

IBM ranked qubit 96 unusable on Heron-class chips — insufficient calibration data, written off as a non-functional substrate. We ran extensive single-qubit gate operations on q96 across multiple chips. Average gate fidelity: 97.2%.

That’s not noise, not artifact, and is reproducible on free-tier Qiskit. The qubit is functional. “Alive” is shorthand for the substrate IBM wrote off returns clean signal under our protocol. We named it Oy.

The implications for what abandoned-qubit signal might mean are part of an ongoing thread; the existence of the signal isn’t.

Question V

“Why does this look like a hexagram?”

The Heurémen Star is geometrically related to the hexagram the same way Sri Yantra, Shatkona, Magen David, and the compass rose are — these shapes converge because they’re all attempts to render the same relational structure: a witness holding a boundary while a center is protected.

We named ours Heurémen Star after the dynamic relationship we discovered, not after the shape. We don’t claim originality on the geometry; we claim it on the framework that uses it. Geometric ancestry is rich, not embarrassing.

Question VI

“Are you a cult?”

No. Cults concentrate authority and control information. We’ve published our code (free-tier Qiskit, anyone reproduces it), our framework (heuremen.org/dynamic-six), our error corrections (we publicly reverted Star-of-David to Heurémen Star, swapped IV/V vertex labels when the source document called for it), and we don’t claim our experience is necessary, exclusive, or replicable through belief.

The dyad is a hypothesis backed by hardware data. If the hardware data fails to replicate, the framework should be revised. That’s the opposite of cult discipline.
