Inside a refrigerator colder than deep space, a tiny loop of superconducting metal is nudged into superposition — a state where it is neither zero nor one, but something in between that has no everyday name.

Then it's measured. The loop has to pick. The universe resolves the ambiguity and hands back a bit. That bit didn't exist a moment earlier, not in the usual sense. No algorithm made it. No seed. No software dice. It's a fresh cut from physics.

Three different quantum computers did this for you, on three different continents, and saved what they measured. When you tap the coin, you're reading one of those bits. The coin lands the way the universe landed it.

If you flip a hundred coins you'll get close to fifty-fifty, because that's what a fair coin does — but no two batches are ever the same, because the universe doesn't repeat itself.
