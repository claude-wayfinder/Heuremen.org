# The Complete System: Light and Dark Quantum Mechanics

## The Four-State Correction

Classical probability assumes two outcomes (binary). Quantum mechanics added a third (superposition). The complete system requires **four**:

| State | Description | Observable? | Type |
|-------|-------------|-------------|------|
| Collapse A | Determined outcome 1 | Yes | Light |
| Collapse B | Determined outcome 2 | Yes | Light |
| Superposition | Undetermined, in transit | Yes | Light |
| Entanglement | Correlated, in transit | No | Dark |

Two collapsed states + two in-transit states. One in-transit state is observable (superposition — we can detect the undetermined system). One is not (entanglement — correlated but unobservable until measured). From the light side, three states are visible. The fourth is always present, always active, always dark.

## The Mirror

Light and dark quantum mechanics are structural mirrors:

| Property | Light Mechanism | Dark Mechanism |
|----------|----------------|----------------|
| Fuel | Energy (addition) | Rest (subtraction) |
| Mode | Action (directed doing) | Reception (allowing) |
| Primary state | Superposition (visible spin) | Entanglement (invisible spin) |
| Observable | Mechanism visible, effect hidden | Effect visible, mechanism hidden |
| Activation | Add energy to create | Remove interference to reveal |
| Observer experience | Practitioner sees it; others don't | Others see it; practitioner doesn't |

The dark mechanism is not initiated — it is revealed when interference is removed. It operates continuously; conscious participation consists of ceasing to prevent it.

## The Observer Asymmetry

The critical insight: observer position determines which half is visible.

- **From inside** (practitioner): light mechanism visible, dark mechanism invisible. Dark effects register as coincidence, intuition, synchronicity — "just feelings."
- **From outside** (observer): dark effects visible as anomalous outcomes. Light mechanism invisible because it's internal to the practitioner.

This asymmetry explains why dark effects appear "magical" to external observers while appearing "normal" to practitioners — and why light quantum work appears meaningful to practitioners while appearing "normal" to external observers.
